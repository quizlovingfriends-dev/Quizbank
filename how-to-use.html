METHOD // DOCUMENT 001

How To Quiz

A short lesson on the only thing that matters: lists.

STEP 01 // TRY THIS

Answer this question.

Which Indian Prime Minister was assassinated in 1984?

ANSWER
Indira Gandhi
STEP 02 // THE TRUTH

You didn't learn a fact.
You discovered a list.

The wrong way to learn this: "Indira Gandhi was assassinated in 1984." One floating fact. Easy to forget. Useless for the next 100 related questions a quizmaster might ask.

The right way: the moment you saw "Indian Prime Minister, 1984", your brain should not have searched for a single name. It should have pulled up THE FULL LIST OF INDIAN PRIME MINISTERS and counted to find who was in office.

Once you have that list at the tip of your tongue, you can answer fifty questions instead of one. Who succeeded her? Who came before? Who was the first? Who served the longest? Who was non-Congress? All the same list.

STEP 03 // THE LIST

Here is the list.

Memorize it in order. Not their dates. Not their parties. Just the sequence. Dates and parties become easy once the sequence is locked.

Look at the list. Notice Indira appears twice (positions 5 and 8). Notice Vajpayee appears at 13 and 16. Notice the Gandhi pattern: Indira → Rajiv. These structural facts are free knowledge once you see the list.

STEP 04 // WHY IT WORKS

Why lists beat facts.

REASON 01
If you forget item #5, items #4 and #6 act like bookends and pull it back into memory. Isolated facts have no bookends.
REASON 02
One list answers dozens of question variations: first, last, longest, shortest, before, after, only one to ___, etc. 1 list = 50 facts.
REASON 03
Lists have structure — order, pattern, repetition, gaps. Your brain remembers structure far better than scattered datapoints.
REASON 04
When a new question comes, you don't recall — you look up the slot. Faster, more accurate, less anxious.
STEP 05 // YOUR JOB

How to build your own.

  1. Every time you miss a question, ask yourself: "What list does this fact belong to?" The answer is almost always sitting on Wikipedia under "List of ___".
  2. Write the list down. A notebook. A Notion page. A text file. Doesn't matter. The act of writing it commits it. Looking it up doesn't.
  3. Memorize sequence first, details second. First just the names in order. Once locked, then add dates, then add parties, then add quirky facts. One layer at a time.
  4. Recite the list daily for a week, then weekly. Without looking. Out loud, ideally. This is how the list moves from "I can find it" to "tip of the tongue."
